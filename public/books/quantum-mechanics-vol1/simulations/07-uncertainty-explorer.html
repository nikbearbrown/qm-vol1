Widen the packet in x and it narrows in p, and conversely — but the product σxσp never moves: the Gaussian sits exactly on the Kennard bound ℏ/2. Lengths in nm, momenta in units of ℏ/nm (so ℏ = 1 and the bound is 1/2).

Why does the product stay pinned at ℏ/2? Because the Gaussian is the unique minimum-uncertainty state — the one state for which the Cauchy–Schwarz step in the Robertson proof holds with equality. Every other shape (try the infinite-well states in the companion Robertson-boundary explorer) sits strictly above the bound. Changing k₀ shifts the momentum distribution to ⟨p⟩ = ℏk₀ without changing either width.
